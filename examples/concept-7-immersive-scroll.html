The Lost Observatory

Somewhere above the treeline, where the air thins and the sky remembers every star it has ever held, there is a door that no one has opened in forty years.

Chapter I

The Map

It arrived in an envelope with no return address -- a hand-drawn chart on paper so old it felt like holding a dead leaf. Contour lines traced a mountain I had never heard of, and at the summit, a single circle drawn in red ink.

The postmark was illegible. The handwriting was my grandfather's. He had been dead for eleven years.

Chapter II

The Journey

Three days on foot past the last road. The forest gave way to scree, and the scree to wind. My compass spun lazily, as though the mountain itself was magnetic, pulling every needle toward its peak.

On the second night I heard something I could not explain -- a low, resonant hum that seemed to rise from the stone beneath my tent. It stopped exactly at midnight.

Chapter III

The Discovery

The dome appeared through the fog like the skull of some vast, sleeping creature. Copper green with decades of weather, its shutters still partially open -- a frozen eye gazing at a sky it could no longer see clearly.

The door was unlocked. Inside, dust motes turned in shafts of grey light, and at the center of the room, the telescope stood upright and waiting, as if someone had only just stepped away.

On the desk: a logbook. The last entry was dated the night my grandfather disappeared.

Chapter V

The Return

I descended the mountain carrying the logbook and a single photograph I found pressed between its pages -- my grandfather, young, standing beside the telescope with a look of quiet astonishment.

The observatory is still there. The door is still unlocked. And on clear nights, if you know where to look, you can see its dome catch the moonlight like a signal meant for someone who has not yet arrived.

Chapter IV

The Stars

I pointed the telescope where his logbook said and turned the brass focusing ring until the blur resolved into light. Not one star, but a cluster -- a tightly packed family of suns that no catalogue had ever named. He had found them. He had told no one.

In the margin of the logbook, in pencil so faint I nearly missed it, he had written a single word: home.
